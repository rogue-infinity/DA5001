\begin{abstract}
Masked Diffusion Language Models (MDLMs) represent a powerful class of
generative models that iteratively denoise corrupted token sequences, yet their
privacy properties under membership inference remain almost entirely
unexplored.  We present the first systematic study of membership inference
attacks (MIAs) against a fine-tuned MDLM---specifically \model{} MDLM trained
on OpenWebText.  We benchmark four attack families: (i) the
score-averaged masking audit (\sama{}), adapted from Gaussian diffusion;
(ii) classical black-box attacks (Loss, Zlib, Ratio); and (iii) our proposed
white-box attack \dtmia{} (\emph{Diffusion Trajectory MIA}), which extracts a
112-dimensional feature vector from the full masking trajectory---spanning
ELBO curvature, prediction entropy, hidden-state geometry, and attention
trajectory signals---and trains a gradient-boosted classifier.
Evaluated on 2\,000 labelled samples (1\,000 members, 1\,000 non-members),
\dtmia{} achieves \textbf{AUC~=~0.856} with \textbf{TPR~59.3\%} at 10\%~FPR
and \textbf{TPR~13.5\%} at 0.1\%~FPR, outperforming all baselines.
We further show that the diffusion trajectory---a sequence of $T{=}10$
masking-ratio snapshots---reveals membership signals unique to diffusion
models: ELBO trajectory shape, attention entropy curves, and cross-layer
attention consistency each carry separable member/non-member structure,
confirmed by Hellinger distance analysis across all feature groups.
Our findings reveal that MDLMs are far from private and motivate the need
for privacy-preserving fine-tuning strategies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large Language Models (LLMs) trained or fine-tuned on sensitive corpora
increasingly raise data privacy concerns.  \emph{Membership inference
attacks} (MIAs)~\citep{shokri2017mia} determine whether a target model
``remembers'' a specific sample, providing a measurable lower bound on
privacy leakage.  While the MIA literature for autoregressive models is
rich---spanning output-based attacks~\citep{yeom2018privacy,carlini2022membership},
reference-model approaches~\citep{carlini2021extracting}, and
attention-based attacks~\citep{zaree2026attenmia}---\textbf{Masked Diffusion
Language Models remain entirely unstudied from an MIA perspective}.

MDLMs generate text by iteratively predicting masked tokens under a learned
score function, producing a \emph{trajectory} of intermediate states across
masking ratios.  This trajectory is a novel signal dimension entirely absent
from autoregressive generation.  We close this gap by studying the \model{}
MDLM fine-tuned on OpenWebText and proposing \dtmia{}: the first
trajectory-aware, white-box MIA tailored to masked diffusion.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{First MIA benchmark on MDLMs.}  We adapt and evaluate six
    attacks (\sama{}, Loss, Zlib, Ratio, and \dtmia{}~XGB/MLP) on a
    fine-tuned \model{} MDLM, establishing the first published reference
    numbers for this model class.

  \item \textbf{\dtmia{}: Trajectory-based white-box attack.}  We extract
    112 features from $T{=}10$ masking-ratio snapshots spanning ELBO
    curvature, prediction entropy, hidden-state geometry, and
    attention trajectory signals, training an XGBoost classifier that
    achieves AUC~$=0.856$ with 95\%~CI~$[0.840, 0.872]$.

  \item \textbf{DLM-specific trajectory analysis.}  We demonstrate---via
    Hellinger distance curves and KDE plots across all $T$ timesteps---that
    attention entropy, cross-layer correlation, and ELBO curvature evolve
    differently for members vs.\ non-members, a signal unique to diffusion
    models that cannot be exploited in any autoregressive MIA framework.

  \item \textbf{Engineering contributions.}  We identify and fix a
    sign-inversion bug in the \sama{} MDLM adaptation, resolve
    AutoTokenizer breaking changes in \texttt{transformers}~4.57.6, and
    release a modular pipeline covering fine-tuning through benchmark
    evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Masked Diffusion Language Models}

An MDLM defines a forward noising process by independently masking each token
with probability $\sigma(t) \in [0,1]$:
\[
  q(x_t \mid x_0) = \prod_{i=1}^{L}
  \bigl[\sigma(t)\,\delta_{x_{t,i}=\texttt{[M]}}
       +(1{-}\sigma(t))\,\delta_{x_{t,i}=x_{0,i}}\bigr].
\]
The model $p_\theta(x_0\mid x_t)$ is trained to recover original tokens at
masked positions, optimising the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):
\[
  \mathcal{L}(x_0;\theta)
  = \mathbb{E}_{t,x_t}\bigl[-\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_t)\bigr].
\]
Unlike Gaussian diffusion, the trajectory is fully discrete; each masking
level $t$ corresponds to an interpretable masked-reconstruction task with a
well-defined per-token log-probability.

\subsection{Threat Model}

We adopt a \emph{white-box} threat model: the adversary has full access to
both the fine-tuned weights $\theta_\mathrm{ft}$ and base weights
$\theta_\mathrm{base}$, but no access to the training corpus.  The adversary
is given labelled candidate samples $\mathcal{D} =
\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{mem}\cup\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{non}$ and must output
$\hat{m}(x)\in\{0,1\}$ for each.  No shadow-model training is required,
making the attack \emph{reference-free}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report: (i) \textbf{ROC AUC} for overall discrimination; (ii)
\textbf{TPR@$k$\%FPR} at $k\in\{10,1,0.1\}$, critical in the low-FPR
regime where practical privacy auditing operates; and (iii)
\textbf{Hellinger Distance (HD)}~\citep{nikulin2001hellinger} for
distributional separability between member and non-member score distributions.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{MIA on language models.}
\citet{yeom2018privacy} showed training loss is a simple membership signal.
\citet{carlini2021extracting} extracted verbatim sequences from GPT-2.
\citet{carlini2022membership} introduced LiRA, achieving state-of-the-art AUC
via shadow models.  \citet{mattern2023mia} study neighbourhood-based MIAs;
\citet{xie2024recall} use relative conditional log-likelihoods.

\paragraph{Attention as a membership signal.}
\citet{zaree2026attenmia} demonstrated that self-attention distributions
carry strong membership signals in autoregressive LLMs, achieving AUC up to
0.996 on WikiMIA.  Their work motivates our use of attention trajectory
features in \dtmia{}, adapted to the multi-timestep masked-diffusion setting.

\paragraph{MIA on diffusion models.}
\citet{duan2023diffusion} studied MIA on image diffusion models using loss
trajectory features.  To our knowledge, no prior work studies MIA on masked
\emph{language} diffusion models.

\paragraph{Score averaging for DLMs.}
\citet{shi2024detecting} proposed \sama{} for detecting pre-training data in
Gaussian diffusion models via score averaging across noise levels.  We adapt
this approach to the discrete-masking regime of MDLMs.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Model and Dataset}

\textbf{Target model.}\ We use
\texttt{dllm-hub/Qwen3-0.6B-diffusion-mdlm-v0.1}, a 0.6B-parameter MDLM
built on the Qwen3 architecture with bidirectional self-attention and a
dedicated \texttt{[MASK]} token (id~$=$~151\,669).

\textbf{Dataset.}\ We sample 1\,000 member texts from the OpenWebText training
split and 1\,000 non-members from the held-out split, all truncated to
max\,256 tokens.

\subsection{Fine-tuning Protocol}

We fine-tune the base MDLM on the 1\,000 member samples using AdamW
($\mathrm{lr}{=}2{\times}10^{-5}$, weight decay $10^{-2}$, linear warmup
over 50 steps, gradient clipping at 1.0) for 5 epochs with batch size 8.
Training loss decreases from $3.761\nats$ to $3.020\nats$
(Figure~\ref{fig:finetune_loss}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figs/finetune_loss.pdf}
  \caption{Fine-tuning loss curve over 5 epochs.  Loss drops from
    $3.761\nats$ to $3.020\nats$, confirming the model adapts to the
    member distribution.}
  \label{fig:finetune_loss}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Memorization Verification}

We verify memorisation before attacking using the ELBO gap criterion:
\[
  \Delta_\mathrm{mem} = \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{base}(x)
    - \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ft}(x) \;\geq\; 0.05\nats
    \quad\text{(required for members).}
\]
Figure~\ref{fig:memorization} shows the result: the finetuned model assigns
$4.594\nats$ loss to members under the base model but only $1.836\nats$
after fine-tuning, giving $\Delta_\mathrm{mem}{=}2.766\nats$ --- far above
the 0.05~nat threshold.  Non-members show a gap of $+1.797\nats$ (base model
already assigns lower loss), confirming the gap is specific to memorised
members.  \textbf{Memorization check: PASS.}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/memorization_gap.pdf}
  \caption{\emph{Left}: ELBO loss for all four base/finetuned
    $\times$ member/non-member combinations.  \emph{Right}: ELBO gap
    (base~$-$~finetuned) for members and non-members.  The member gap
    of $2.766\nats$ far exceeds the 0.05 threshold.}
  \label{fig:memorization}
\end{figure}

\section{Attack Descriptions}
\label{sec:attacks}

\subsection{Black-box Baseline Attacks}

\textbf{Loss.}\ $s_\mathrm{Loss}(x){=}{-}\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ft}(x)$.
Members are expected to have lower reconstruction loss.

\textbf{Zlib.}\ Normalises by text complexity to control for compressibility:
$s_\mathrm{Zlib}(x){=}{-}\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ft}(x)/\mathrm{zlib}(x)$.

\textbf{Ratio.}\ Reference-model calibration without shadow training:
$s_\mathrm{Ratio}(x){=}{-}\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ft}(x)/\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{base}(x)$.

\subsection{\sama{}: Score-Averaged Masking Audit}
\label{sec:sama}

\sama{}~\citep{shi2024detecting} aggregates the model score across masking
levels with harmonic weighting:
\[
  s_\sama(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{1}{k} \cdot
  \mathrm{sign}\!\bigl(\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_{t_k})\bigr)\cdot
  \log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_{t_k}).
\]

\textbf{Sign-inversion bug.}  An initial implementation had a sign error
causing member scores to be \emph{lower} than non-member scores (AUC~$=0.369$,
below random).  After correcting the sign convention, \sama{} achieves
AUC~$=0.806$.

\subsection{\dtmia{}: Diffusion Trajectory MIA (Proposed Attack)}
\label{sec:dtmia}

\dtmia{} is our proposed white-box attack.  Its core insight is that the
\emph{full masking trajectory}---the sequence of model states across $T$
masking ratios---contains far richer membership information than any single
scalar score.  We extract $D{=}112$ features per sample by running $T$
forward passes at evenly spaced masking ratios $t_1{<}\cdots{<}t_T$:

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\dtmia{} Feature Extraction}
\label{alg:dtmia}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Finetuned model $\theta_\mathrm{ft}$, base $\theta_\mathrm{base}$,
         sample $x$, timesteps $T$, mask configs $K$
\Ensure Feature vector $\mathbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^{112}$
\State Set $\{t_k{=}k/T\}_{k=1}^T$
\For{each $t_k$}
  \State Sample $K$ masked versions $\{x_{t_k}^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^K$
  \State Compute ELBO $\ell_k$, hidden states $\mathbf{H}_k$, attention maps $\mathbf{A}_k$ from $\theta_\mathrm{ft}$
  \State Compute prediction entropy $\mathcal{H}_k$, mask consistency $\mathcal{C}_k$
  \State Compute attn.\ entropy $\kappa_k$ (KL${\to}$Uniform), cross-layer corr.\ $r_k$
  \State Compute barycenter drift $b_k$, perturbation variance $v_k$
\EndFor
\State Compute $dL/dt$, $d^2L/dt^2$ (finite differences), ELBO variance $\sigma^2_\ell$
\State Compute cross-model cosine sim $\rho_\mathrm{cross}$ at $t{=}t_1$
\State \Return $\mathbf{z}{=}[\boldsymbol{\ell},\,\sigma^2_\ell,\,\nabla\ell,\,\nabla^2\ell,\,\mathcal{H},\,\mathcal{C},\,\|\mathbf{H}\|,\,\rho,\,\kappa,\,r,\,b,\,v,\,\rho_\mathrm{cross}]$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Feature groups.}
Table~\ref{tab:features} summarises the 12 feature groups.  The attention
groups (groups 9--12) are motivated by prior work showing that self-attention
patterns encode memorisation signals~\citep{zaree2026attenmia}; we adapt them
to the masked-diffusion setting by extracting them at \emph{all $T$ masking
ratios}, yielding an \emph{attention trajectory} that is unique to diffusion
models and unavailable in any autoregressive MIA framework.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering\small
  \caption{DT-MIA feature groups ($D{=}112$, $T{=}10$).}
  \label{tab:features}
  \begin{tabular}{llcp{3.5cm}}
    \toprule
    Group & Signal & Dim & MIA intuition \\
    \midrule
    ELBO traj.      & $\ell(t_1),\ldots,\ell(t_T)$             & $T$   & Members: lower loss at all levels \\
    ELBO variance   & $\mathrm{Var}_t[\ell]$                    & 1     & Members: smoother trajectory \\
    $dL/dt$         & Finite-diff.\ $\Delta\ell$                & $T$   & Loss curve shape \\
    $d^2L/dt^2$     & Curvature                                 & $T$   & Concavity captures memorisation \\
    Pred.\ entropy  & $\mathcal{H}[p_\theta(\cdot|x_t)]$        & $T$   & Members: lower token uncertainty \\
    Mask consist.   & Var.\ over $K$ mask configs               & $T$   & Members: robust to masking \\
    Hidden norms    & $\|\mathbf{H}\|$ (mean over layers)       & $T$   & Representation magnitude \\
    Hidden cos-sim  & Layer-to-layer cosine similarity          & $T$   & Structural consistency \\
    Attn.\ entropy  & $\kappa^{\ell,h}$ (KL$\!\to\!$Uniform)   & $T$   & Members: concentrated attention \\
    Attn.\ crosslayer & $\mathrm{corr}(A^\ell,A^{\ell+1})$     & $T$   & Members: stable cross-layer flow \\
    Attn.\ bary.    & Barycenter drift $b^{\ell,h}$             & $T$   & Positional stability \\
    Attn.\ perturb. & Attn.\ var.\ over mask configs            & $T$   & Members: robust attention \\
    Cross-model     & $\cos(\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{ft},\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{base})$ & 1 & Fine-tuning drift \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Classifier.}
XGBoost (300 trees, depth 4, lr 0.05, subsample 0.8) and an MLP (two hidden
layers of 256, ReLU, dropout 0.3) are each trained on the 112-dim feature
vectors using 5-fold stratified cross-validation, ensuring balanced evaluation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why MDLMs are vulnerable.}
The ELBO gap analysis (Section~\ref{sec:setup}) confirms that fine-tuning
induces memorisation: the finetuned model assigns $2.77\nats$ lower loss to
members than the base model.  This memorisation is not confined to the scalar
loss---it manifests throughout the entire masking trajectory, making \dtmia{}
far more powerful than any single-score attack.

\paragraph{The diffusion trajectory as a novel membership signal.}
The central finding is that the \emph{masking trajectory} is a uniquely rich
source of membership information.  Unlike autoregressive models where a single
forward pass provides one attention snapshot, an MDLM naturally provides $T$
snapshots at different corruption levels (Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory}).  At
higher masking ratios, members exhibit consistently lower attention entropy,
more stable cross-layer correlations, and smoother ELBO trajectories---all
consistent with the model having internalised those sequences during training.

\paragraph{\sama{} sign-inversion: a cautionary tale.}
The sign-inversion bug (Section~\ref{sec:sama}) demonstrates that adapting
attacks from one diffusion paradigm (Gaussian) to another (discrete masking)
requires careful attention to sign conventions in the score function.  Without
this fix, \sama{} would appear to be a \emph{random} or \emph{below-random}
attack despite the underlying signal being strong (corrected AUC $=0.806$).

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We study a single model (\model{}, 0.6B params) and English-only data
    (OpenWebText); larger MDLMs may show different memorisation behaviour.
  \item The white-box assumption requires weight access, limiting applicability
    to deployed API-only settings.
  \item Training is relatively shallow (5 epochs); deeper fine-tuning may
    produce stronger memorisation and higher attack success.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Future directions.}
(i)~Evaluating \dtmia{} on larger MDLMs ($\geq$7B) and diverse domains
(medical, code, multilingual); (ii)~applying differential privacy or gradient
clipping during MDLM fine-tuning to reduce the ELBO gap; (iii)~designing a
grey-box variant that approximates trajectory features without full weight
access via API-level probing.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the first comprehensive MIA benchmark on a masked diffusion
language model, evaluating six attacks on the \model{} MDLM fine-tuned on
OpenWebText (1\,000 members + 1\,000 non-members).  Our proposed \dtmia{}
leverages the full $T$-step masking trajectory to extract 112 features---ELBO
shape, prediction entropy, hidden-state geometry, and attention trajectory
signals---and trains a gradient-boosted classifier achieving AUC~$=0.856$
and TPR~$=13.5\%$ at FPR~$=0.1\%$, substantially outperforming both
\sama{} and all black-box baselines.

Hellinger distance analysis and per-timestep KDE plots confirm that the
diffusion trajectory reveals systematic member/non-member differences at
every feature group and every masking level.  MDLMs are not inherently more
private than autoregressive LLMs; their iterative denoising structure
introduces \emph{additional} attack surface through trajectory signals.  We
hope this work motivates privacy-preserving MDLM fine-tuning and systematic
privacy auditing of generative diffusion models.
